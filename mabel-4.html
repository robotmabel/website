01 — System

A body, composed.

Every joint earns its place. From the swerve modules under the chassis to the rotational neck and twin 17-DOF hands, MABEL is engineered as one coherent platform — not a stack of subsystems.

Active perception

A head that
looks where it
thinks.

3-DOF rotational neck (pan, tilt, roll) carries a stereo RGB-D camera and an animated LED matrix face. The head doesn't just see — it expresses, signaling intent the way a person does before they act.

Stereo RGB-D 3 DOF neck LED matrix eyes
Reach & posture

A torso that
changes its mind.

Integrated standing-desk lift gives MABEL real vertical range — from floor pickup to countertop hand-off. A tilt joint lets it lean in to look, lean back to think. A 13″ touchscreen turns the chest into a face when the eyes aren't enough.

Linear lift Torso tilt joint 13″ touchscreen
Dexterous arms

Seven joints
per side.

Twin 7-DOF arms based on the Open Arm reference — backdriveable, payload-capable, and tuned for bimanual coordination. Eye-in-hand wrist cameras give MABEL the close-up vision the head can't always provide.

2 × 7 DOF Wrist cameras Open Arm derived
ORCA hands

17 degrees of
fingertip care.

Sixteen finger joints plus an active wrist roll. The ORCA hand lets MABEL pinch a coin, grasp a coffee cup, knock on a door, and fold a t-shirt — without swapping end-effectors. Soft fingertip pads. Tactile-ready.

16 finger DOF Active wrist roll Tendon driven
Holonomic base

Three swerves.
Any direction.

A three-module independent swerve drive — adapted from First Robotics — gives MABEL true holonomic motion. It can strafe through a doorway, rotate in place to face you, and hold a heading while it moves. 2D LiDAR underneath powers SLAM.

3 × swerve modules 2D LiDAR Onboard mini PC
02 — Capabilities

What it actually does.

Six capabilities, one platform. Each was a research project somewhere. Here, they're table stakes.

A

Bimanual fine manipulation

Both hands cooperate on a single object. Fold a shirt, twist open a jar, hand off a tool.

B

Long-horizon autonomy

Plan multi-step tasks across rooms and floors. Diffusion Policy stitched to SLAM-based navigation.

C

Building-scale mobility

Elevator buttons, doors, corridors. The swerve base composes with active perception to handle real interiors.

D

Spatial teleoperation

Apple Vision Pro streams hand pose and gaze directly to MABEL with sub-100ms latency over local Wi-Fi 6.

E

Expressive HRI

LED matrix eyes and touchscreen torso make state legible — what MABEL sees, where it's going, what it'll do next.

F

Onboard learning

Teleop sessions stream straight to HDF5/Zarr for IL pipelines (ACT, Diffusion Policy). No external rig required.

G

Edge inference

The entire stack runs on a single Jetson Thor onboard. No cloud, no laptop tether.

H

Open hardware

Every CAD file, every gerber, every URDF. Reproduce or remix — that's the contract.

Detail

Up close, honest.

02 · Hands

Tendons,
not gears.

Each ORCA finger drives through Dyneema-cored tendons routed through PTFE-lined channels in the palm. Backlash near zero. Repair time measured in minutes, not days.

04 — Teleoperation

Wear the goggles.
Be the robot.

A custom visionOS app turns Apple Vision Pro into MABEL's nervous system. Hand pose, finger curl, head orientation, and gaze stream directly to the robot over local WebRTC.

You move.
It moves.

No joystick. No keyboard. Pinch to grasp, raise your arm to lift, look where you want MABEL to look. Every teleop session auto-logs to HDF5 — every frame, every joint angle, every camera, every action — straight into the IL pipeline.

01
Hand-to-hand mapping

26-joint hand tracking from visionOS maps onto the 17-DOF ORCA hand with retargeting solved per finger.

02
Sub-100ms loop

WebRTC over local Wi-Fi 6 keeps the perception-to-actuation latency where it needs to be for embodied immersion.

03
Data-collection by default

Every demo doubles as a labeled trajectory. Compatible with ALOHA/ACT and Diffusion Policy training out of the box.

05 — Long-horizon demo

Laundry. From 28 to 6.

One task, twelve sub-steps, two elevators, three doors, and one floor of a Manhattan high-rise that didn't know a robot was coming. Every step runs autonomously, stitched end-to-end.

Step 01 · 00:00

Picks up the laundry bag.

Bimanual lift from the bedroom floor. Diffusion Policy chooses the grasp; the second hand stabilizes the bag's weight transfer onto the lift column.

Apt · floor 28 · bedroom
Step 02 · 00:42

Navigates to the elevator.

SLAM-based global plan; the holonomic base strafes around the kitchen island, holding heading so the wrist cameras keep the bag in view.

Apt corridor · floor 28
Step 03 · 01:15

Presses the elevator button.

Active perception locates the call button. A single finger from the ORCA hand extends, presses, retracts. MABEL waits.

Elevator lobby · 28F
Step 04 · 02:08

Enters the elevator. Selects floor 6.

The base rotates to clear the doorframe and strafes in. The arm reads the floor panel, finds 6, and presses. The doors close.

Elevator car
Step 05 · 03:30

Exits, navigates to the laundry room.

Pre-built floorplan, live SLAM correction. MABEL recognizes the laundry-room door, approaches, and pauses for the handle.

Laundry room · 6F
Step 06 · 04:12

Opens the door. Loads the machine.

Bimanual: one hand holds the door, the other opens the washer lid, lifts the bag, transfers clothes. Closes the lid. Selects the cycle. Pays.

Washer · loaded · paid
Step 07 · 06:48 · complete

Returns to the apartment.

The reverse path. Eyes go from amber-attentive to soft-cyan-idle. MABEL parks, lowers its torso, waits for the next task.

Apt · floor 28 · idle

Cost is a design parameter, not an excuse.

Every component on this list was chosen with two questions in mind: does it work for the task, and could a grad student in Lagos, São Paulo, or Beijing buy it next week?

3D-printed structural parts. Off-the-shelf actuators where they're good enough. Custom electronics only where they have to be. No proprietary couplings, no five-figure encoders, no "contact sales" components.

The result is a research-grade mobile bimanual robot that one person can afford and one weekend can assemble. That's the contract.

08 — Build it

One weekend.
One robot.

Full assembly takes a single, focused weekend with two people. Print, screw, flash, ship.

Print the structural parts.

~ 36 print-hours · PETG or ABS

STLs ship pre-oriented for a Bambu X1C-class printer. Total filament mass: ≈ 2.1 kg. We've validated prints at 0.2mm layer, 25% gyroid infill.

Wire the swerve base.

~ 3 hours · two people

The base assembles as three identical swerve modules around a single power-distribution PCB. Connectors are keyed; you cannot wire it wrong.

Assemble the arms & hands.

~ 5 hours

Each arm and ORCA hand follows the published Open Arm and ORCA build guides, with a MABEL-specific torso bracket included in this repo.

Mount the head & torso.

~ 2 hours

The lift column bolts directly to the swerve base; the torso slides onto the lift and clips into place. The head mounts on the neck via four M3 screws.

Flash firmware. Pull the repo.

~ 1 hour

One docker compose up brings up the entire ROS 2 stack. Calibration is automated — MABEL stretches each arm through a sequence on first boot.

Ship it.

~ 5 minutes

Connect Vision Pro. Open the MABEL app. Pinch the air. The robot moves. Welcome to research.
